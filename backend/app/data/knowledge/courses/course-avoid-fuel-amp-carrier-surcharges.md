# Avoid Fuel &amp; Carrier Surcharges

**Avoid Fuel & Carrier Surcharges**

Fuel and carrier surcharges can add hundreds of dollars to the cost of your "free" flight. This unit explains how to avoid paying them.

In this training unit, we will cover...

## What are fuel and carrier surcharges?

Whenever you redeem frequent flyer points for a reward flight, you'll also need to pay any applicable taxes, fees and fuel surcharges (also known as carrier charges or insurance surcharges).

Government taxes and airport fees are payable on any airline ticket, regardless of whether it's a revenue or award booking. But not all airlines impose fuel surcharges!

Fuel surcharges and carrier charges are directly payable to the airline, and are not a real tax. They're really just a cash grab by the operating airline, and they can add hundreds of dollars to the cost of your reward ticket.

For example, Qantas Frequent Flyer adds $1,050 in "carrier charges" on a return Qantas Business Classic Reward ticket from Australia to Europe. After adding on all other taxes, this means you would pay a total of 332,600 Qantas Frequent Flyer points + well over $1,500 in taxes & carrier charges for a return Qantas Business Classic Reward ticket from Sydney to London!

## Airlines with no or low fuel surcharges

The amount of fuel surcharges payable on award tickets will depend on both your frequent flyer program and the airline operating your flight. You will (almost) never pay fuel surcharges if you redeem your points on an airline that doesn't charge them - regardless of the frequent flyer program being used.

## Other airlines

Other airlines that charge no/low fuel surcharges on their own flights include Singapore Airlines, United Airlines, Hawaiian Airlines, South African Airways and Avianca.

## Frequent flyer programs that don’t pass on fuel surcharges

Some frequent flyer programs don't pass on fuel surcharges at all, even if you're booking a flight on an airline that would normally charge them!

This applies to frequent flyer programs including:

- American Airlines AAdvantage (Oneworld)

- Alaska Airlines Mileage Plan (Oneworld) - a flat USD12.50 partner award fee applies instead

- United MileagePlus (Star Alliance)

- Avianca LifeMiles (Star Alliance)

- Air Canada Aeroplan (Star Alliance) - a flat CAD39 partner airline booking fee applies instead

This is great news as it means you can **redeem AAdvantage or Mileage Plan miles for Qantas flights**, for example, and** not pay any of Qantas' usual carrier charges**. In fact, you can redeem AAdvantage miles to fly with any Oneworld airline and you'll only pay fuel surcharges for flights with British Airways or Iberia, (which are two exceptions to this rule).

Pay no fuel or carrier surcharges on most partner airline redemption bookings made through Alaska Airlines Mileage Plan. Photo: Alaska Airlines.

By redeeming Aeroplan points on Emirates, you can even get around Emirates' notoriously high carrier charges. However, Aeroplan uses dynamic pricing on Emirates award (opposed to fixed pricing on partners such as Virgin Australia or Singapore Airlines), which often means you'll need more points.

You may also be able to avoid paying high fuel surcharges by booking tickets from places where these are (or have been) regulated such as Brazil, the Philippines or Hong Kong.

## The UK Air Passenger Duty

Flights departing from London Heathrow and other UK airports are subject to the Air Passenger Duty tax. Photo: Matt Graham.

Another way to avoid paying extra taxes (on any flights, not just award bookings) is to avoid booking flights that originate in the UK.

The UK government charges an Air Passenger Duty (APD) on most flights departing from the UK. The exact amount depends on the class of travel and distance flown, but can be as high as £224 (~AU$473) per passenger!

Here is a list of Air Passenger Duty rates as of 1 April 2025:

RouteEconomyPremium cabinsUK Domestic£7£14Short-haul international flights (to countries with a capital city up to 2,000 miles away)£13£28Medium/long-haul flights (2,000-5,500 miles)£90£216Ultra long-haul flights (over 5,500 miles)£94£224

If you departing the UK on a short-haul flight that connects to a long-haul flight, you will still pay the long-haul rate based on the final destination on your ticket.

## How to avoid the UK Air Passenger Duty

There are a number of ways to avoid paying the Air Passenger Duty:

- The obvious method is to avoid flying out of the UK. It's often cheaper to take the Eurostar train to Paris, Brussels or Amsterdam and fly back to Australia from there - even via the UK (see the point below).

- The tax is not charged if you're transiting through the UK on a single ticket for under 24 hours

- Long-haul flights departing Northern Ireland (e.g. Belfast) are exempt, and there is a reduced charge for departures from Scotland

- Children under 16 are exempt if flying in Economy

**The APD is not charged when arriving in the UK.** So if you're planning a holiday to Europe that includes the UK, aim to arrive in the UK and then depart from continental Europe. ;)
