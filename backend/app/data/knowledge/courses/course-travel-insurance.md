# Travel Insurance

**Travel Insurance**

Travel insurance is essential for any overseas travel. This unit explores the most cost-effective ways to ensure you're covered, including for COVID-19.

In this training unit, we will cover…

## The importance of travel insurance

When planning an overseas trip, purchasing travel insurance is probably the most important step! As the saying goes...

If you can't afford travel insurance, you can't afford to travel!

Travel insurance is simply invaluable if something goes wrong during your trip. If you were to become sick or have an accident while overseas, you could be up for thousands of dollars in medical bills and other expenses. In fact, hospital bills in the United States can add up to literally *millions* of dollars! Having suitable travel insurance means that you won't have to worry about this.

Of course, travel insurance doesn't just cover medical costs. It also has you covered if your bags go missing, if you get stranded somewhere, and in many other situations where you could be left out of pocket due to unforeseen events that can happen when you're travelling.

The dangers of travelling overseas without travel insurance simply cannot be ignored. If you end up in an overseas hospital without travel insurance and can't afford to pay the bills, you could be refused life-saving treatment. The Australian government will not pay your medical costs if you've failed to purchase travel insurance.

## Types of cover

There are a range of different types of travel insurance cover:

- **Single-trip** insurance covers only one return trip

- **Annual multiple-trip** insurance is the best option for frequent travellers that make lots of short overseas trips throughout the year.

When taking out travel insurance, **always check that the level of cover is appropriate**. Read the Product Disclosure Statement (PDS) and make sure that you're covered. Many policies have various exclusions, e.g. for people over a certain age or with certain pre-existing medical conditions.

Beware also that some policies won't cover events that happen as a result of you being intoxicated.

In general, travel insurance does not cover trips to countries with a "Do not travel" warning issued by the Australian government's Smartraveller website. Some insurers also do not cover destinations marked as "reconsider your need to travel", but this varies.

If you want to search for specific information within a long PDS document, use your computer's search function to search for relevant keywords.

## Travel insurance & COVID-19

Most (but not all) Australians travel insurers now offer some level of cover for COVID-19. However, the level of coverage differs between policies.

Some insurers will only accept claims if you are personally diagnosed with COVID-19, and not if you need to isolate as a close contact or your trip is disrupted because a travel companion got COVID-19. Furthermore, some insurers may only cover COVID-19 if you have a positive PCR test result - not accepting rapid antigen tests.

## What does COVID-19 travel insurance cover?

COVID-19 travel insurance **generally covers the following scenarios**:

- The cost of medical treatment and/or repatriation if you contract COVID-19

- Accommodation costs if you are forced to quarantine due to contracting COVID-19 or denied boarding due to having symptoms

- Cancellation costs if you or your travel companion contracts COVID-19 in Australia and can’t travel, or a relative/business partner contracts COVID-19 and their condition is life threatening

COVID-19 insurance does **not** generally cover the following:

- Government-imposed border closures, lockdowns & travel bans

- Travel to countries classified by DFAT as “Level 4 – Do Not Travel” (and in some cases, "Level 3" countries)

- Multi-night cruises

- Quarantine that you already knew would be required

- The cost of COVID-19 tests required for travel

- If you are not vaccinated or ignore public health advice such as hygiene & social distancing guidelines

- If you decide not to travel due to concern about COVID-19

## Australia's reciprocal health care agreements

Keep in mind that Australia also has reciprocal health care agreements with 11 countries:

- Belgium

- Finland

- Italy

- Malta

- Netherlands

- New Zealand

- Norway

- Ireland

- Slovenia

- Sweden

- UK

See the Services Australia website for details of the agreements, which vary by country.

If you have an Australian Medicare card, you can access some level of free emergency health care in these countries. So, it's really important that you carry your Medicare card with you!

This doesn't negate the need for travel insurance, which also covers you for many other things that could go wrong. But it's an extra layer of protection when you travel to these countries.

## When to purchase travel insurance

**The best time to purchase travel insurance is usually as soon as possible after you've booked your flights.**

Your travel insurance doesn't just cover you during your trip. It also covers you before you travel, in case something happens and you need to cancel the trip. By buying travel insurance sooner rather than later, you're actually getting covered for longer at no extra cost.

Travel insurance policies won't generally cover you for events that were known at the time of purchase. For example, most travel insurance policies won't cover travellers affected by volcanic eruptions in Bali if the volcano began erupting after the policy was bought. So buying the policy sooner is always better.

Finally, beware that some insurers will not cover cancellations due to COVID-19 if you purchase your policy within 21 days of departure.

## Credit card travel insurance

Many Australian credit cards come with complimentary travel insurance. This can be a useful benefit, but may not necessarily give you the cover you need.

These are generic policies that are bought cheaply and in bulk by the credit card provider. Some credit card insurance policies come with exclusions that invalidate the policy for reasons you might not expect. So always read the PDS carefully.

Beware that you may need to purchase flights or spend a minimum amount towards your trip using your credit card in order to activate the insurance.

You should also be aware of the following:

- Only the primary cardholder, supplementary cardholders or eligible family members travelling with a named cardholder are generally covered

- To be covered, you generally need to have a return ticket back to Australia when you start your trip (i.e. one-way trips aren't covered) 

- Most credit card policies have age limits and trip duration limits

- Some pre-existing medical conditions or certain types of activities (e.g. adventure sports) may not be covered. Some insurances offer paid upgrades to cover more activities, pre-existing conditions, etc.

AFF has published a handy guide to credit card travel insurance which includes an overview of coverage offered by the major banks.

## Coverage for lost frequent flyer points

Most travel insurance policies will reimburse you for lost frequent flyer points or award flight bookings if you are unable to recover your costs in any other way and your insurance covers travel delay/cancellation costs.

In some cases, the insurer will reimburse actual frequent flyer points (e.g. Qantas Insurance policies can give you back your Qantas points). Others will reimburse the cost of buying replacement top-up points. But in most cases, you'll get back the equivalent cost of a retail airfare.

Check the PDS of your policy to see what coverage is available.

## Domestic travel insurance

If you're an Australian resident, travel insurance is not as vital for domestic travel within Australia compared to international travel. That's because you'll still be covered by Medicare and/or your health insurance for any medical issues that may arise while you're still in Australia.

But domestic travel insurance can still be useful because it will cover many other things that could go wrong while travelling, such as missing baggage, rental vehicle excess, and so on.

If you have a credit card with travel insurance, this may provide adequate cover for domestic travel. But you should check the PDS.
