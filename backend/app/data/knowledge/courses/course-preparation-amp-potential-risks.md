# Preparation &amp; Potential Risks

**Preparation & Potential Risks**

There are many benefits to buying frequent flyer points. But there are some risks, and some preparation is required before jumping into the deep end.

In this training unit, we will cover...

## Preparing to buy points

In the next module of this course, we'll feature a range of overseas frequent flyer programs that sell points or miles. Each of these programs has different pros & cons, and are best used for different types of redemptions.

British Airways Executive Club could be a good program to join if you want to fly Qatar Airways Business Class. Photo: Qatar Airways.

As you make your way through this course, think about:

- Where would you like to fly to using your points?

- In which class of travel would you like to fly?

- Are you flexible with travel dates?

Your answers to these questions will determine which program/s you should target.

Once you've decided which programs you might like to use, you can prepare by signing up for those programs (it's usually free to join). Then, you'll be ready to start buying points whenever you need them and a good promotion comes along.

## Potential risks (and how to mitigate them)

There are some risks to buying points. But most of these can be mitigated with a little bit of planning.

Here are some of the most common problems you may encounter, and how to reduce the risk.

## Problem 1: Reward seat availability is limited

When redeeming frequent flyer points, you can't just use them to book any flight. There must be reward availability on the flight you wish to book.

Availability is rarely a problem for domestic or international Economy redemption bookings. You might not get a seat on the *exact* flight you want, but there are usually a few suitable options.

By contrast, premium cabin reward seats on long-haul flights can be extremely limited - especially during peak periods like school holidays. This is a big problem the majority of people buying points are doing so precisely to redeem them for these premium cabin long-haul flights. It is not uncommon to find no Business or First Class reward seats on the flights you wish to book, particularly on flights between Australia and North America.

**Solution: Check for reward seat availability before buying points**

Plan your trips well in advance, so that you're ready to act when new promotions on buying miles are announced. When this happens, always check that the flights you wish to book are available before committing to buying the miles. Once the miles have reached your account, book the flights immediately.

If you can't find any award availability in your chosen frequent flyer program, try using some of the tricks discussed in Course 5 (Finding & Booking Reward Flights). And if you still can't find any award seats, you may need to look for a new frequent flyer program.

## Problem 2: Some programs exclude "new members" from buying miles

This varies between loyalty programs, but some won't let you buy miles if you:

- Joined less than 1-2 months ago,

- Have no existing points in your account, or

- Were not already a member before the current promotion was announced

**Solution: Sign up for each program in advance!**

The first step is to actually join each of the overseas frequent flyer programs featured in this course!

If you haven't already done so, join each of these programs now. They're all free to join and it takes only a few minutes. You'll find links to sign up for each featured program below.

Don't delay this step! Even if you're not sure whether you'll use the program, you should sign up now because new members are often excluded from special promotions. In fact, some programs don't allow new members to buy miles at all.

We don't *just *recommend signing yourself up. Why not sign up your entire family? Each account you have increases the likelihood of getting a great targeted bonus offer, and you'll be able purchase more miles each calendar year.

If you think you might want to buy points from a program that requires a minimum balance in your account, you might also want to find a way to get some points into that account ASAP.

## Problem 3: Points can lose their value at any time

There is a chance that your points will lose some of their value after buying them.

Airlines can increase the cost of award flights any time. Usually they'll provide advance notice of any changes, but not always. Remember that we're dealing with overseas frequent flyer programs, so Australian rules and regulations don’t necessarily apply.

**Solution: Avoid stockpiling points**

It's generally best to buy points with a specific redemption in mind, and to redeem them straight away. If you later discover that you are not able to use the miles, you won't be able to get a refund and they may end up expiring.

Despite the risks, there are some exceptions where it can make sense to stockpile points. For example, British Airways Avios are great for last-minute Qantas domestic bookings, so maintaining a balance of these could make sense. Purchasing more points than you need may also give you access to a better discount.

## Problem 4: The frequent flyer program may go out of business

If you happened to have any points with Ansett when they went out of business, you'll know that if the airline collapses you could lose all of your points. More recently, when Airberlin filed for bankruptcy, Airberlin Topbonus miles because virtually worthless.

airberlin Topbonus miles became worthless after the airline went bankrupt. Photo: Matt Graham.

**Solution: Again, avoid stockpiling points**

Again, this is a risk that you take when you have points sitting in a frequent flyer account. However, your booking should be safe if you've already redeemed the points for a flight on an airline that's still operating before the frequent flyer program goes out of business. The frequent flyer program will have already paid the operating airline for the ticket so it would remain valid.

## Problem 5: Annual mileage purchase limits

Most frequent flyer programs limit the number of miles that you may purchase in any calendar year. This could be a problem if the reward you want to book costs more than the annual purchase limit.

**Solution: Buy miles at the end of the year**

If you purchase the annual maximum amount on 31 December, your limit will reset the next day. So you can buy the extra points needed and book your award flight.

Other possible solutions include using a family member's account to buy the extra miles (if the program allows mileage transfers/booking flights for other people) or using a program without an annual purchase limit, such as Alaska Airlines Mileage Plan.

## Problem 6: Exchange rate fluctuations

As we are dealing with overseas programs, you'll need to pay for points using foreign currency. Transactions are usually in US Dollars (USD). You may also need to pay the award booking taxes in USD when redeeming the miles.

Exchange rates fluctuate, so points can become cheaper or more expensive overnight. Generally, buying miles works out to be a better deal when the Australian Dollar (AUD) is high against the USD.

**Solution: Monitor exchange rates and pay using a card with no forex fees**

If you plan to buy miles (or anything else from overseas) regularly, consider opening a credit card that does not charge forex or international transaction fees.

## A bonus is not equivalent to a discount!

Finally, it may be tempting to compare an offer of 50% bonus miles to a 50% discount. But these are not the same! A 50% discount is the same as 100% bonus miles.

For example, if miles normally cost $30/thousand and you get a 50% discount, you'll pay $15 per 1,000 miles. But with 50% bonus miles, you'd be getting 1,500 miles per $30 spent. That's a higher cost of $20 per 1,000 miles.
