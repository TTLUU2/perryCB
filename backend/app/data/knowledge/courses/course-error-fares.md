# Error Fares

**Error Fares**

The cheapest fares are sometimes published by mistake! This unit explains how you can find error fares, and what to do if you find a great deal that seems too good to be true.

In this training unit, we will cover…

## Types of error fares

As the name suggests, error fares are published by mistake.

With millions of fare combinations out there, and humans involved in putting them out there, it is inevitable that mistakes are made occasionally. There are probably thousands of error fares out there right now that nobody even knows about!

If you see an error fare, you could of course get yourself a great deal. However, airlines don't *always* honour these prices.

These are the most common types of error fares:

## Fares filed in the wrong currency

Sometimes airline staff accidentally file a fare in the wrong currency. This can work in your favour (or not), depending on the exchange rate. For example, a fare filed in Malaysian Ringgits using US Dollar figures would be around 75% off! Similarly, airlines may take time to adjust to large, sudden changes in exchange rates.

## Fat fingers

This could happen where a price is missing a zero or there is a typo in the price. For example, an airline employee could type "$1,500" instead of "$5,100".

## Fares missing one or more components

This could affect airlines that add large fuel surcharges to their fares. For example, Qantas removed fuel surcharges as an airfare component in July 2015. This change took place overnight and there was a delay before base fares were increased to compensate. This led to return flights to Los Angeles selling for under $500!

For half a day, Qantas sold hugely discounted tickets to long-haul destinations. Photo: Qantas.

For anyone interested, this is the principle on which "fuel dumping" works. It also presents some interesting opportunities when buying tickets originating in countries where fuel surcharges are regulated (e.g. Brazil or the Philippines).

## Incorrect class of travel

It's also possible that an economy fare might be incorrectly loaded into a Business fare bucket. Many airlines use different fare class codes for the same types of tickets, so it's possible that one airline's economy code is business on another airline, and so on. This can cause some confusion and human error is possible.

## How to find error fares

The easiest way to find your own error fares is to run lots of "flexible destination" searches through Google Flights. There are probably thousands of error fares out there right now that nobody knows about. So if you search long enough, chances are you'll find something interesting.

But if you don't have hours to spend, there are ways to be notified about error fares that other people have found. The easiest way is to subscribe to websites that send "cheap flight" alerts. Scott's Cheap Flights is a good one, but there are many others. It also pays to regularly check online forums like Australian Frequent Flyer and Flyertalk.

## Fuel dumping

This trick is complicated and very difficult to take advantage of. But in case you're interested, there is a concept known as "fuel dumping". This is where the fuel surcharge component of an airfare is removed.

You may notice that many airfares include a fuel surcharge. This may equate to hundreds of dollars. Fuel dumping seeks to trick the ticketing system into removing this fuel surcharge, leading to a saving of hundreds of dollars.

One potential way to do this is to add an extra flight to the booking, on a random carrier that doesn't impose fuel surcharges. You may never take this extra flight. But as long as the extra flight, sometimes known as the "third strike" is the last sector on the booking, the risk of having the ticket cancelled is relatively low.

In reality, it's extremely difficult to find a combination of flights where this will work. It's also questionable as to whether this practice is ethical. For these reasons, we don't encourage this. If you wish to do further research on this topic, do so at your own risk.

## What to do if you find an error fare

Once enough people have discovered the error fare, the airline will usually pull it from sale quickly. These deals often last just hours and there's no guarantee that the fare will be honoured.

So, what should you do if you discover a mistake fare?

DO...

- Book immediately

- Take screenshots of the booking process and confirmation

- Pay by credit card (as the payment goes through instantly and you also have a chargeback option down the track)

- Make sure you receive a confirmed e-ticket

- Wait before making other bookings, e.g. hotels

DON'T...

- Call the airline!

- Describe it as an error/mistake fare

- Book via an Online Travel Agent (unless this is the only place the fare is available)

- Plan the rest of your holiday until you're sure the fare will be honoured

## Risks when booking error fares

There are a few **key risks** involved in booking error fares:

- The airline might cancel your ticket

- If the airline does not cancel your ticket, expect to travel exactly as booked or not at all

- Cancellation fees are often higher than the actual fare

- To change your booking, you'll need to pay the entire fare difference

There is a high chance the ticket will be cancelled by the airline. Usually this happens within the first week after booking. But either way, wait a few days before making non-refundable hotel or other related bookings. Travel insurance won't cover you, and the airline won't compensate you for other bookings if your ticket is cancelled.

Having said that, airlines do sometimes honour these fares. That's why it can be worth taking the risk and booking.

If it's already been two weeks and the ticket still shows as confirmed, it's probably safe. Sometimes airlines make public statements as to whether or not the deal will be honoured, so that's also a good guide. And if it was a well-publicised fare, there's probably an online discussion about it on the Australian Frequent Flyer or other forums.

## Consumer protections on error fares

If the airline cancels a mistake fare you've purchased, is there anything you can do about it?

Consumer protections available with error fares will vary according to the country where you bought the ticket. But generally, airlines are not legally obliged to honour very obvious mistake fares. You can take up the issue with the small claims tribunal (or similar), but honestly, you're not likely to be successful. In this game, you win some and lose some. It's best to wait for another deal rather than flogging a dead horse.

Airlines are not legally obliged to honour very obvious mistake fares.

That said, many airlines do honour error fares to avoid bad publicity and generate goodwill. But it depends on how big the discount was and how many people booked. Clearly it would be financially disastrous to honour the fares if 10,000 people booked a $50 First Class ticket to London.
