# Why Buy Frequent Flyer Points?

**Why Buy Frequent Flyer Points?**

Fly in luxury for less by buying frequent flyer points! This unit explains the principles of this strategy.

In this training unit, we will cover...

## Welcome to our "Buying Points" course

Frequent flyer points can be redeemed to travel all over the world! But before you can start booking flights, you first need to earn the points.

If you travel a lot, spend a lot on your credit cards and maximise the many offers out there, you can earn quite a lot of points. But if you need more points to book the flights you want, it's also possible - and potentially quite lucrative - to buy frequent flyer points.

It's against the rules of most frequent flyer programs for individuals or third parties like mileage brokers to sell points. However, some frequent flyer programs sell their points directly to members. This makes it possible - and completely within the loyalty program rules - to buy frequent flyer points in bulk.

This opens up some very interesting opportunities. By buying frequent flyer points, it's possible to travel in the pointy end of the plane - that's right, in Business or First Class - for literally a fraction of the price!

In the first module of this course, we'll explain what you need to know about the strategy of buying miles, including how it works and some of the risks involved.

In the second module, we'll show you how to buy and redeem points or miles with the best value frequent flyer programs. Some of these open the door to great value domestic flights, while others allow you to save thousands on international flights. You'll be flying in the pointy end before you know it!

## Why buying points can be great value

Buying points is lucrative when the cost of the points is lower than the value of the reward flight you're redeeming them for. As an example, you might purchase $1,000 worth of points and redeem them for $3,000 worth of flights. That's a great deal!

The basic principle of buying points is to acquire them at the lowest possible cost. You should them aim to redeem the points for high-value rewards, such as Business or First Class tickets.

The trick is to know *when* and *from which airline/s *to buy the points. You then need to find the sweet spots in the relevant program, to redeem those points for maximum value.

Buying points will not *always *work out cheaper than buying a ticket. In fact, for Economy Class redemptions, the cost of the points will often be higher than the cost of simply buying an airfare. But buying points for premium cabin tickets - which are often priced 5x or even 10x higher than Economy fares - can be extremely lucrative.

## Discounts and bonus points

Many of the frequent flyer programs we'll be looking at offer regular promotions to encourage members to buy more points or miles. (Note that as some programs use "points" and others "miles", we're using these terms interchangeably.)

Promotions can include:

- Discounts on miles purchases

- Bonus miles

- Combinations of both discounts and bonus miles

The trick to obtaining points at the lowest cost is to **wait for a promotion**. Some airlines run "buy miles" promotions almost monthly, while others are only once or twice per year. The next module covers to the typical offer amounts and frequency of each of the featured programs.

Most programs impose a limit on the number of points that can be purchased in a single calendar year. If you're planning to purchase the maximum amount, it's best to wait for an especially good promotion.

## How it works

Buying and redeeming points is **a win-win for passengers, loyalty programs and airlines**. Here's why...

When you buy miles and redeem them for a flight, the frequent flyer program will make a margin on the redemption. The frequent flyer program will also pay the operating airline for that seat.

The amount that the operating airline makes for a redemption booking is typically less than what it would charge a full-fare paying customer. But as award availability is capacity controlled, seats made available as frequent flyer redemptions are generally seats the airline did not otherwise expect to sell. So, the airline is still making more money than it otherwise would have by letting the seat fly empty.

In the example below, a customer purchases Air Canada Aeroplan points and redeems them to book a seat on Lufthansa. Lufthansa is paid for the seat by Aeroplan, and the customer has paid Aeroplan for the points. As a customer, you'll still be ahead as long as the price you paid for the points is lower than the regular Lufthansa ticket price.

## Examples of lucrative redemptions

There are many flights for which buying points works out cheaper than buying a ticket. Here are just a few examples of the savings on offer…

## Qantas Business Class from Sydney to Perth for $608 (78% off)

Qantas A330 Business Class. Photo: Qantas.

Qantas normally charges $2,796 for a one-way Business Class ticket from Sydney to Perth, flying in a lie-flat A330 Business Class suite.

Airfares from Sydney to Perth on the Qantas website.

But by buying American Airlines AAdvantage miles during a 40% discount promotion, you could buy enough miles to book a seat for just USD387 (~AU$578).

It would cost USD387 to buy 20,000 AAdvantage miles at 40% off.

You'd then just pay around USD20 (~AU$30) in taxes when redeeming your miles, bringing the total cost to around $608. (There are no fuel or carrier surcharges when redeeming AAdvantage miles for Qantas flights.)

An example of an AAdvantage award seat from Sydney to Perth on the American Airlines website.

## Lufthansa First Class from San Francisco to Frankfurt for $2,392 (~85% off)

Lufthansa's original A380 First Class cabin. Photo: Lufthansa.

It costs 100,000 Air Canada Aeroplan points, plus around AUD85 in taxes, to fly from San Francisco to Frankfurt in Lufthansa First Class. (You can generally book Lufthansa First Class tickets using partner airline points or miles within 3 or 14 days of departure.)

Reward seats from San Francisco to Frankfurt available to book with Air Canada Aeroplan points.

During a regular promotion with Aeroplan offering 70% bonus points on purchases, you could buy the required Aeroplan points for around AUD2,307. Compared to the regular airfare of AUD19,457 that's a huge discount!

Lufthansa First Class airfare from San Francisco to Frankfurt on Google Flights.

## EVA Air Business Class to Taipei for a similar price to Economy

Sometimes buying points can nab you a Business Class ticket for around the same price as an Economy ticket!

In this example, a one-way EVA Air "Economy Basic" ticket from Brisbane to Taipei would normally cost around $954:

An example of EVA Air BNE-TPE airfare pricing on the airline's website.

But by buying Avianca LifeMiles during a typical 145% bonus promotion, you could buy 50,000 LifeMiles for a cost of around AUD1,000. You could then redeem those miles, plus around USD68 (~AU$102) to fly Business Class for a total cost of around $1,100. That's barely more than the cost of an Economy ticket, and around $2,000 off the usual Business Class airfare!

An EVA Air Business Class award available on the LifeMiles website.

These are just a few examples. Buying points can help you save thousands of dollars on many more premium cabin airfares all over the world, including on flights to Europe, Asia and North America. You'll see plenty more examples throughout the rest of this course!
