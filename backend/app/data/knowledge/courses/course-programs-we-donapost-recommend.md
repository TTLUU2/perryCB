# Programs We Don&apos;t Recommend

**Programs We Don't Recommend**

Not all frequent flyer programs are worth joining. These programs offer relatively poor value.

## Why some frequent flyer programs should be avoided

Unfortunately, not all frequent flyer programs offer great value. Some offer very poor value or are best avoided for other reasons.

You're likely to come across many. of the programs featured in this unit at some point. Indeed, you can transfer points earned from Australian credit cards into these programs.

There are so many frequent flyer programs out there which offer genuine value and are highly competitive. So, there's no need to waste your time with programs that offer sub-par value!

This unit explains why the frequent flyer programs of Air New Zealand, Malaysia Airlines, Emirates and Thai Airways are not the best options out there. This is not to say that these are poor airlines - in fact, they are all very good airlines to fly with - they just don't have particularly competitive loyalty programs.

If you're flying with any of these airlines, consider crediting the flights to a partner airline's frequent flyer program instead. For example, you could choose to credit Malaysia Airlines flights to a Oneworld frequent flyer program such as Qantas Frequent Flyer, British Airways Club or American Airlines AAdvantage, instead of the airline's own Enrich program.

Having said that, there are some benefits in sticking with your own airline's frequent flyer program if you happen to fly with the same airline often. For example, it may be easier for you to upgrade and you may have access to better status benefits as a member of the airline's own program. This is something to consider if you mainly fly with one airline.

## Air New Zealand Airpoints

Photo: Matt Graham.

Air New Zealand's Airpoints program has a moderate presence in Australia. But it almost has a monopoly in New Zealand - which might explain why the program is not competitive. In its home market, where the majority of Airpoints members live, it simply doesn't have to be.

Airpoints Dollars, the program's currency, are treated like cash. There are no award charts for Air New Zealand flights because redemptions are based on the cash ticket price. This works in a similar way to Qantas' poor-value Points Plus Pay awards. Yet, Airpoints Dollars can't be used to pay for taxes & surcharges on international flights.

You can redeem Airpoints to fly on partner airlines, but the award chart for this is poor value and bookings are not easy to make. Distances on the partner airline award chart are based on kilometres (rather than miles, which is the industry norm) and all partner awards are priced as one-way, point-to-point flights. If there are connecting flights, you'll pay full price for each individual leg.

That said, there are pockets of good value on the partner airline award chart - such as point-to-point First Class flights on Star Alliance partners like Lufthansa.

The earn rates for flying are almost as bad, with some Air New Zealand flights earning just *one *Airpoints dollar.

You can use your Airpoints to request an upgrade, but there are no fixed-price upgrades on Air New Zealand. Instead, you'll have to bid for a "One Up" upgrade using money, Airpoints or both.

And to top it off, Airpoints Dollars expire after 4-5 years - regardless of your account activity.

We would only recommend using the Airpoints program if you mainly fly within New Zealand, on Air New Zealand. Many domestic Air New Zealand flights (especially cheaper flights) will not earn any points if credited to most partner frequent flyer programs.

There are* *some useful benefits for regular Air New Zealand flyers that travel enough to earn elite status, including free upgrades. Air New Zealand does treat its elite members well. But unless Air New Zealand is your main airline, we would recommend steering clear of the Airpoints program. You may consider other Star Alliance programs instead, such as United MileagePlus (which generally allows you to earn miles on all Air New Zealand fares).

## Key facts: Airpoints

- **Airline:** Air New Zealand

- **Currency:** Airpoints Dollars

- **Alliance:** Star Alliance

- Status is earned by accumulating Status Points

- **Status levels:** Silver, Gold, Elite

- **Airpoints Dollars expire** after 4-5 years

- **Our program rating:** 1.5/5

## Malaysia Airlines Enrich

Photo: Matt Graham.

Numerous Australian credit card providers offer Malaysia Airlines Enrich as a transfer partner. But we would not recommend transferring points into the Enrich program.

For a time, Enrich was actually a very good loyalty program with some great value redemptions. Sadly, it's no longer very... well... enriching.

But over the past decade, the award charts have been devalued excessively. Redemptions on Malaysia Airlines are now revenue-based (similar to Airpoints), and partner airline awards are prohibitively expensive. This is especially the case for long-haul flights.

The Oneworld multi-carrier award is no longer offered.

If you wanted to use points to fly on Malaysia Airlines, you'd be better off redeeming through another Oneworld program (such as Qantas Frequent Flyer or Cathay). The only time it would make sense to use Enrich is if it was the only frequent flyer program with award availability on a flight you wanted.

That said, Enrich does offer some nice status benefits if you are a regular flyer on Malaysia Airlines.

## Key facts: Enrich

- **Airline:** Malaysia Airlines

- **Currency:** Miles

- **Alliance:** Oneworld

- Status is earned by accumulating Elite Points

- **Status levels:** Silver, Gold, Platinum

- **Miles expire** after 3 years

- **Our program rating:** 2/5

## Emirates Skywards

Photo: Matt Graham.

Emirates may be a world-class airline, but its frequent flyer program Skywards is uncompetitive on the global stage.

Skywards is a transfer partner of Amex Membership Rewards and CommBank Awards, although the transfer rates are generally poor.

Despite the poor transfer rates, the miles are not particularly valuable. Many award flights require a high number of miles and Emirates seems to make it difficult to actually use your Skywards miles, especially when redeeming on partner airlines.

There are some bright spots in the Skywards program. For example, there is often good availability of Flex Plus awards in Business & First Class on Emirates flights. However, Emirates' fuel surcharges are among the highest of any airline. So, you could expect to pay quite a high number of Skywards miles - plus around $3,000-4,000 in taxes & charges - for a round-trip Emirates Business reward ticket from Australia to Europe.

Emirates Business Classic Reward (Flex Plus) pricing on the SYD-LHR route. Screenshot from the Emirates website.

Emirates may have cheaper Saver award seats available on some flights, but you can only book these as part of a round-trip booking (unless departing from Australia or New Zealand).

If you want to redeem for Emirates First Class flights, the only ways to do this are through the Emirates Skywards, Qantas Frequent Flyer or Air Canada Aeroplan programs.

## Key facts: Skywards

- **Airline:** Emirates

- **Currency:** Miles

- **Alliance:** Emirates is not in an alliance

- Status is earned by accumulating Tier Miles

- **Status levels:** Silver, Gold, Platinum

- Miles expire after 3 years

- **Our program rating:** 2.5/5

## Thai Airways Royal Orchid Plus

Photo: Matt Graham.

Thai Airways Royal Orchid Plus is a transfer partner of Amex Membership Rewards in Australia. It used to be a fairly good frequent flyer program with some valuable Star Alliance redemptions. But in October 2019, Thai Airways massively increased the cost of award flight redemptions, including Star Alliance and round-the-world awards. The program is now a mess and awards are terrible value.

Furthermore, during the COVID-19 pandemic, Thai Airways suspended all redemptions on Star Alliance airlines for more than three years. And miles expire after three years, regardless of your account activity.

If you have Amex points and want to transfer them to a Star Alliance program, choose Singapore Airlines KrisFlyer instead.

## Key facts: Royal Orchid Plus

- **Airline:** Thai Airways

- **Currency:** Miles

- **Alliance:** Star Alliance

- Status is earned by accumulating Qualifying Miles

- **Status levels:** Silver, Gold, Platinum

- **Miles expire** after 3 years

- **Our program rating:** 1.5/5
