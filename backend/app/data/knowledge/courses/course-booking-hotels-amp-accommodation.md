# Booking Hotels &amp; Accommodation

**Booking Hotels & Accommodation**

This unit explores the process of finding a good hotel and getting the best value on your room rate.

In this training unit, we will cover…

## Identifying hotel options

The first step in searching for hotel rooms at your destination is to identify and narrow down your options.

The easiest way to do this is by searching on one of the many hotel booking aggregator websites such as Trivago, Booking.com or even just Google Travel. These sites present an overview of options and allow you to filter and sort the results. But they might not display every hotel!

The cheapest hotels may be outside the city centre or in a nearby town. Unless you specifically need to stay in the city centre, you could save substantial money by widening your search area.

If you want to use your hotel loyalty points, a good place to start looking could be rooms.aero. This website is affiliated with seats.aero and can help to identify reward night rooms available through the major hotel loyalty programs.

## Checking reviews

Once you've narrowed your options down to 2-3 hotels, a logical next step is to check online reviews. Websites like Tripadvisor can be useful as they contain a large number of reviews written by other travellers. These reviews contain insights you won't find advertised by the hotel.

However, online reviews could be biased or written by people with unrealistic expectations. That's why it's important to take online reviews with a pinch of salt!

Probably the best way to tell if a hotel is a good choice is to check the overall breakdown of ratings. If the majority of ratings are either "excellent" or "very good", then it's probably a safe choice:

If the hotel has quite a few 1 or 2 star ratings, it's worth reading some of these negative reviews to find out why that is. If there is a recurring theme, then it's probably true. But some people have a habit of leaving 1-star reviews for minor service issues that you may not consider important.

For example, we've seen a 1-star review for a hotel in Greece due to the night receptionist speaking poor English. This kind of review is not that helpful and says nothing about the quality of the hotel.

Also be cautious of highly positive reviews. Of course, the reviewer may have just been very happy. But you should be wary of hotels with large numbers of 5-star reviews that have been written by people with only 1 review to their name. This is especially suspicious if the reviews all have a similar writing style. It's a possible sign that the reviews were written by someone that works at the hotel (we have seen several examples of this before).

## Finding the best rate

Once you've chosen a hotel, make sure you're getting the best rate!

The same room doesn't always cost the same price on every booking website. Before booking, search for your hotel on an aggregator website such as Trivago or Hotels Combined to see where to book at the lowest price.

You should also **check the price of booking direct on the hotel's own website**. It may or may not be cheaper than the best rate found on Trivago or Hotels Combined.

## Cancel and re-book for a better rate

One strategy for ensuring you always get the best rate is to book a flexible rate directly with the hotel in advance. Then, keep an eye on rates leading up to your stay (especially around two weeks out). If the price goes up, it means you booked at the best price. If the price goes down, you can cancel and re-book at the lower price!

This strategy works because most hotel bookings can be cancelled in advance for no penalty.

If the price drops before your stay, cancel and re-book at the lower rate!

Don't forget that you'll need to book direct with the hotel if you wish to earn hotel loyalty points or use your elite status benefits with that hotel!

## Resort fees

If you're travelling to North America, you may come across "resort fees". These are essentially the **hotel equivalent of fuel surcharges**, and they're a total rip-off. They are increasingly prevalent in the United States and Canada, but (thankfully) are rare elsewhere. In Australia, they would be considered drip-pricing and would be illegal.

Some North American hotels charge mandatory resort fees on top of the advertised nightly rate. Hotels generally claim that their resort fees cover the use of facilities such as the swimming pool, shuttle services, gym or valet parking.

The charges originated in luxury resorts that actually offered services worth paying extra for, but the concept has even now been adopted by budget hotels in the United States. Some hotels have started to include the use of basic features of the room, such as the safe or even the bathtub, as part of the resort fee. We even found hotels in the United States that add a $30/night charge which only covers access to the internet.

The actual fee amounts vary from hotel to hotel, but are typically between $20-40 per night. Some hotels charge a flat fee per stay. An easy way to find out whether a hotel charges a resort fee – and if so, how much it is – is to search on ResortFeeChecker.com.

There's not much you can do about resort fees, other than to:

- avoid hotels that charge them, or

- ask for the resort fee to be waived at the hotel reception if you haven't used any of the services included in the resort fee.

## Mystery hotel deals

One way to save big bucks on hotels is to use mystery hotel deals. You won't get to see which hotel you're booking until after paying, so there is a risk the hotel might not be what you expected. There are also no refunds. But if you're prepared to take that risk, the savings can really add up.

There are various websites offering Mystery Hotel deals. These include Hotwire, Agoda, Wotif and lastminute.com.

## Priceline "Pricebreakers"

For something a bit left-field, Priceline offers something called "Pricebreakers". This has replaced its old "Name Your Own Price" offer.

With Pricebreakers, Priceline will show you 3 different hotels in similar locations and a price which is lower than the regular nightly rate at all 3 hotels (it could be up to 50% off).

If you choose to book, you'll pay the advertised price and find out which of the 3 hotels you'll be staying in **after** paying. (Anecdotally, it seems to be the hotel with the lowest normal retail room rate around two-thirds of the time.)

## Get cashback on hotel bookings with Cashrewards

If you're booking accommodation (or any other sort of travel) using an online travel agent, you may be able to get cashback for your booking via Cashrewards!

Cashrewards is free to join, and partners with websites including Booking.com, Expedia, Agoda, Hotels.com, Hotels Combined and Trip.com. Simply click through from Cashrewards to your preferred website, complete your booking, and you'll receive a percentage of the transaction amount as cashback in your Cashrewards account. Once you've accumulated at least $10, you can then transfer your Cashrewards balance to your personal bank account.

Typical cashback amounts are around 3-4%. But during special promotions, you could earn up to 12% cashback (e.g. with Booking.com).

## Alternatives to Hotels

Hotels are not your only accommodation option! There are numerous alternatives, and many offer significant savings over hotel prices. Some are even completely free.

## Airbnb

Photo: Tommy McGrath.

Airbnb is one of the best alternatives to hotels. The popular website connects travellers with local hosts that have a spare room – or even a whole house – that they are willing to rent out to guests.

Stays can be booked easily through the Airbnb website, and the experience is often comparable to staying at a bed n' breakfast. But Airbnb properties are generally cheaper than hotels. Depending on your host, you may also get some handy tips on things to see and do in that city.

Before booking, **be sure to check the reviews**. If the property has a large number of positive reviews, you can be sure of a good experience. However, be wary of properties with only a small number of reviews, or consistently poor reviews.

You can also **earn 1 Qantas point per dollar** by booking Airbnb stays through the Qantas website. But, beware that the prices shown on the Qantas website don't always match those on the Airbnb website. You may be paying a higher price in return for the Qantas points.

## Hostels

Photo: cottonbro studio.

 Another option is hostels. Backpacker hostels aren't for everyone, but there are several advantages…

- They are usually much cheaper than hotels

- Hostels are a great place to meet other travellers

- Many hostels offer extra amenities and services for free or at low cost. e.g. free wifi, breakfast, laundry services and even organised tours and activities

Hostels are aimed at younger travellers, but most will welcome sociable travellers of any age. Even if you'd prefer a private room with an ensuite bathroom, you'll still usually pay less than an equivalent hotel room.

There are plenty of websites for comparing and booking hostels; two of the biggest are Hostelworld and Hostelbookers.

## Couchsurfing

This next option is almost free!

Couchsurfing is an online community that connects travellers with hosts that are willing to offer a spare bed or couch. The site is aimed primarily at backpackers, but anyone can use it. It's ideal for travellers that enjoy meeting new people while saving on accommodation costs.

Couchsurfing does charge a small annual fee of €13.99 (~AUD22) to be a member of their website. But in principle, hosts offer their spare bed for free. Although some travellers choose to buy their host a small gift, or shout them a drink, a simple "thank you" is usually payment enough.

Just like Airbnb, guests as well as hosts can leave reviews about the people they've stayed with. This makes it easy to identify trustworthy hosts and guests alike. And, because the hosts are essentially volunteers, they're usually doing it because they genuinely want to meet other people and make them feel welcome.

## House sitting

Photo: Francesca Tosolini on Unsplash.

One final left-field option is house sitting. This is essentially where you offer to look after another person's home while they are away. In return for the free accommodation, you may be asked to do odd jobs like looking after the owner's pets, watering the plants or taking out the bins.

There is a surprisingly large market for house sitting in Australia and around the world. And thanks to modern-day technology, it's easier than ever to connect homeowners with potential house-sitters.

There are many websites where house-sitting opportunities are advertised. These include:

- Happy House Sitters

- Aussie House Sitters

- Mind A Home

- Mind My House

- Stayz (participates in Cashrewards - get 1.4% cashback)
