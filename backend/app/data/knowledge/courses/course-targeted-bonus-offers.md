# Targeted Bonus Offers

**Targeted Bonus Offers**

Beat the supermarkets at their own game! This unit shows you how to trick the supermarkets into giving you significantly more points and greater savings, without spending more at the checkout.

In this training unit, we will cover…

## Why supermarkets reward disloyalty

If you shop at Woolworths or Coles, scanning your loyalty card each week, you're probably disappointed with the lack of meaningful rewards.

The reason for this is that the supermarket **loyalty programs actually reward disloyalty much more than loyalty**! It may seem like a paradox, but it makes perfect sense the more you think about it.

Loyalty programs claim that they exist to reward loyal customers. This isn't the whole story. In reality, loyalty programs aim to:

- Encourage customers to shop more often

- Encourage customers to spend more per shop

- Target you with personalised marketing and offers

The supermarket loyalty programs want you to scan your card every time you shop. That way, **they can collect personal information about you and your shopping habits**. The programs rely on this data to send you targeted offers. These offers are used to incentivise you to spend more.

If you're a loyal customer, the supermarket doesn't need to send you valuable offers as an incentive to come back. They know you'll continue shopping with them anyway! But **if the supermarket thinks you're spending money with a competitor, it will send you much better offers**. That's how disloyalty is rewarded!

## How targeted bonus offers work

Targeted bonus offers are where 98% of the value in supermarket loyalty programs lies!

You'll normally earn 1 Flybuys point per dollar spent at Coles. But with some offers, this earn rate can increase to more than 50 Flybuys points per dollar!

If you're clever about how you use the Flybuys programs, the majority of points earned can be bonus points! Before you know it, your annual Flybuys review email will look something like this:

Excerpt from a Flybuys email showing the number of points earned on an account in one year.

Exactly the same principle also applies to Everyday Rewards.

The offers these loyalty programs send you are determined by computer algorithms. They are based on the data collected about you and your spending habits by the supermarket.

The supermarket's computers are clever. But you're smarter than a computer and you can trick the system!

Ultimately,** these targeted offers aim to entice you to spend a little more than the supermarket thinks you normally do**. So if you normally spend $50 per week at Coles, for example, you may receive an offer of 2,000 bonus Flybuys points for spending $80 the following week. But it would not make sense for Coles to give the same offer to somebody that usually spends $200 each week. In this case, Coles might offer 3,000 bonus points for spending $250 in the following week.

As a general rule, the spending targets set out by Coles or Woolworths will be $30 to $50 higher than your typical average spend, whatever that may be. For bonuses on purchases from a specific product category, the spend target is usually about $5 to $10 higher than your average.

The trick to getting great targeted bonus point offers is to trick the system.

By making the supermarket think that you spend less than you really do, you'll receive better offers. As we'll discover shortly, this *doesn't* mean you actually need to spend more to take advantage of these offers! You just need to be smart about it.

## Increasing spend, increasing targets

As you take up each offer and increase your average spend to meet the given spending targets, these targets will slowly increase in response.

As you spend more, the supermarkets may gradually increase your offer spend targets. Image: Adobe Stock.

If your spending targets continue to increase, it's best to start ignoring the offers temporarily. If you stop using your card for a little while, or start putting through smaller transactions, the targets will eventually decrease again. Keep in mind that there may be a lag period of a few weeks, up to a month, for the spending targets to adjust.

## Examples of targeted bonus offers

Targeted bonus offers come in many different forms:

- Emails (most common)

- At the bottom of your supermarket receipt

- Paper coupons sent in the mail

- Offers for activation in the Flybuys App

- Boosters in the Everyday Rewards App

Some **examples of targeted offers** include:

- Spend $100 and receive 1,000 bonus points

- Spend $10 on meat and receive 250 bonus points

- Get a 10x points "booster" on entire your shop and/or specific product categories (common for Everyday Rewards)

- 5,000 bonus points for first-time Online orders

- Spend $270 and get 10,000 bonus points

- Spend $50 each week for 4 weeks and receive 10,000 bonus points

The latter types of offers are often the most lucrative:

Examples of targeted offers from Flybuys and Everyday Rewards.

With offers like "spend $50 per week for 4 weeks to earn 10,000 points", you're earning 10,200 Flybuys points for a total spend of just $200. That's 51 points per dollar spent!

When given a choice of cash or bonus Flybuys points as the reward for completing a spend target, you should always pick the bonus points. You can always convert 10,000 Flybuys points to $50 later, if you want, but you can't convert $50 to Flybuys points.

## How to get better offers

Here are 7 expert tips for getting better targeted bonus offers from Flybuys and Everyday Rewards:

## 1. Make sure you've opted-in to email communications

If you're not getting any offers, make sure you've opted in to receiving promotional emails.

## 2. Open multiple loyalty accounts per household

Then, alternate which cards you scan when you shop and/or split the shop between cards. Flybuys and Everyday Rewards both encourage one account per household, but there's no reason everyone in your house couldn't have their own card. If transferring points to Qantas or Velocity, you can use family transfers (or family pooling) to transfer all your family members' points back to your own account… or not. Using this strategy, the supermarket will think you spend less.

## 3. Spend less per transaction

Again, this is to make the supermarket think you spend less per shop. This could involve doing more frequent but smaller shops. Or, just split your transactions half way through. Pay for the remaining groceries with cash and/or a different card, and scan a different loyalty card (or no card) on the second transaction. You will forgo some points initially, but remember that the true value is in the bonus offers!

## 4. Split your shopping between supermarkets

Don't be loyal! Shop at both supermarkets (or even Aldi sometimes, which has no loyalty program and a credit card surcharge). This makes it appear that you spend less in total on groceries. It also gives you the flexibility to shop with whichever supermarket sends the best offer that week.

## 5. Don't scan your card for a while

Some of the best offers are the "we miss you" offers that get sent if you haven't shopped for more than a month!

## 6. Combine offers for even more rewards

You can use multiple offers together unless the offer terms and conditions specify otherwise.

## 7. Alternate payment methods

Supermarkets may track your spend without you even scanning your loyalty card! Your credit card could be linked to your loyalty card if you've ever used them both in the same transaction. So the supermarket can still track your purchase if you pay with your usual credit card but don't scan your loyalty card.

If you have multiple credit cards, keep one card aside that you never use to pay for purchases when scanning your loyalty card. Alternatively, pay cash if you don't want your spending tracked.
