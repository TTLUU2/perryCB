## Travel money options

When travelling overseas, there are a few ways you can pay for your purchases in foreign currency...

When travelling overseas, you'll probably use a mixture of cash and cards for payments. Cash can be useful to have, but cards are more convenient and less risky for larger purchases. You'll also need to use a credit or debit card for things like hotel pre-authorisations and rental car security deposits.

In this unit, we compare the different types of payment cards. In the next unit, we'll take a detailed look at the best ways to obtain foreign currency cash.

## Travel money cards

Banks, airlines and even travel insurance providers are often quick to spruik the benefits of prepaid travel money cards such as Qantas Pay (formerly Qantas Travel Money). That's because these cards are highly profitable for their providers. But they're not always so great for customers!

Most travel money cards offer the same basic features:

- Load foreign currency at a fixed exchange rate before you travel

- Use your card like a debit card overseas to make purchases or withdraw cash from ATMs

These cards can be convenient. But here's why prepaid travel money cards aren't always the best value...

- The exchange rates are often uncompetitive

- Many cards come with hidden fees - although some banks advertise that there are no fees for loading money onto the card, there are often other fees

- It's impossible to know exactly how much you'll spend in advance while on holiday. You'll either load too much or not enough on the card. If you load too much, you'll have to convert the remainder back to Australian dollars - losing more money on the exchange rate in the process.

Even if the card has no fees at all, the bank would still make money by giving you a poor exchange rate.

## Pros & cons

Here are some of the advantages and disadvantages of using travel money cards:

**Pros**

- Earn frequent flyer points with some cards (e.g. Qantas Pay)

- Lock in the exchange rate before you travel (this could work either for or against you, depending on the movement of exchange rates)

- Convenience of a prepaid card

**Cons**

- Should not be used for hotel/rental car pre-authorisations (it is best to use a credit card for this)

- Fees

- Limited range of foreign currencies - most cards offer around 10 currencies

- Poor exchange rates

- No interest earned on the card's balance

## Debit cards with no forex fees

Debit cards are an alternative to travel money cards. You'll still be spending your own money, but there are some key **advantages of using debit cards over prepaid travel money cards**:

- No need to pre-load foreign currency. Just spend or withdraw cash as needed and the amount will be converted at the exchange rate applicable at the time of the transaction.

- Spend in any foreign currency (not just currencies pre-loaded onto your card)

- Earn interest on your account balance while you're travelling

- Some cards have fewer fees and more competitive exchange rates than travel money cards

- Your account can also be used to transfer money to overseas bank accounts.

There are several Australian debit cards that have no foreign transaction fees, no ATM withdrawal fees and no account fees!

These really do give you the best of both worlds as you can access your money overseas while paying no fees. The exchange rates on these debit cards also tend to be around 1% from the spot rate, which is much more competitive than travel money cards.

## Overseas ATM operator fees

While none of the accounts shown above charge ATM withdrawal fees, the ATM operator may charge their own fee.

In any case, it is relatively easy in many countries to find overseas ATMs that don't charge withdrawal fees. This is the case in most of Europe and Japan, though it can vary. Countries where you may have to pay a small fee include the USA, Thailand, Cambodia, the Philippines, Cambodia, Laos, Myanmar and Papua New Guinea.

Most "high street" ATMs in the UK charge no ATM operator fees. Photo: Matt Graham.

Generally, the ATM will warn you before levying a fee (but even then, some have reported it is not actually charged).

Using an ATM operated by a bank, rather than a third-party ATM such as "Euronet", will also increase your chances of avoiding ATM operator fees.

## Credit cards with no forex fees

Although credit cards are not ideal for withdrawing cash overseas, they are generally a good option for purchasing goods and services. Credit cards are also useful in case your hotel or car rental company requires a security deposit or credit card pre-authorisation.

Most credit cards charge a foreign transaction fee of around 3% on overseas purchases. But if you frequently travel overseas - or shop online - it's worth investing in a credit card with no forex fees.

In Australia there are a range of credit cards with no foreign transaction fees. Some of these even come with no annual fees. Others award frequent flyer points on overseas purchases.

## Dynamic Currency Conversion

When making a transaction overseas, you'll sometimes be given the option to pay in Australian dollars. This is known as "dynamic currency conversion". It's a rip-off that is best avoided!

Dynamic Currency Conversion offered at an ATM in Europe. Photo: Matt Graham.

The merchant may claim that paying in Australian dollars is more convenient for you. It isn't.

In reality, the exchange rate will be shockingly awful. On top of this, you may still be charged a foreign transaction fee by your bank as it will recognise the merchant as being overseas.

It's even worse if you've loaded foreign currency onto a travel money card, then pay in Australian dollars. You'll *not only* pay to have the currency converted twice, at questionable exchange rates, but you'll *also* have to pay an additional non-native currency fee!

Always pay in the local currency.

It's always better to pay in the local currency and let your own bank do the conversion. You'll be far better off - especially if you're using a card with no foreign transaction fees.
