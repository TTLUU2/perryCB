# Get the Best Seat on the Plane

**Get the Best Seat on the Plane**

Learn the tricks to scoring the best seat on your next flight.

In this training unit, we will cover…

## Aircraft seat types

Not all aircraft seats were created equal! Some have extra legroom or other benefits, such as extra storage space.

We'll begin by revealing the types of aircraft seats to look out for. We'll then show you exactly how to get the best seats. By using these tricks, you won't need to worry about the seating squeeze - or middle seats - again.

## Exit row seats

Exit row seating on TAP Air Portugal Airbus A320. Photo: Matt Graham.

Exit row seats are highly sought-after because they have extra legroom. They're located next to emergency exits, so there needs to be enough room for passengers to evacuate quickly in an emergency.

There are some disadvantages to sitting in exit rows:

- You will not be allowed to stow your hand luggage under the seat in front. (This may be a good thing as it means guaranteed overhead locker space!)

- Some exit row seats do not recline (this may also apply to seats directly in front of an exit row)

- These seats can become quite cold during the flight

- All passengers sitting in the exit row must be over 15 years old and able to help in an emergency, so they're not an option for families travelling with children.

## Bulkhead seats

Bulkhead seats have a wall in front. Photo: Matt Graham.

Bulkhead seats have a wall in front of them. These seats are usually found in the first row or directly behind a galley area. The main benefit of bulkhead seats is that there is nobody in front to recline into you. You'll also usually get more knee room. Depending on the aircraft, there may or may not be more leg room. Bear in mind, you lose the extra space under the seat in front to stretch your legs.

There are some downsides to bulkhead seats:

- The tray table is often stored inside the armrest, meaning the armrest cannot be raised and the seat is slightly narrower

- If a personal in-flight entertainment TV screen is available, this may need to be stowed away for take-off and landing

- As with exit rows, you'll need to place any carry-on bags into the overhead lockers for take-off and landing.

Bulkhead seats are often reserved for families with small children because the wall can be used to hang a bassinet.

## Front row seats (non-bulkhead)

Front row seats without a wall in front are highly prized as there is usually unlimited legroom. For example, row 4 on the Qantas 737 (front row of Economy class) has the most legroom of any seat on the plane.

Legroom in row 4 on the Qantas Boeing 737-800. Photo: Matt Graham.

## Other special seats

Each aircraft has its own unique seats with desirable features. For example, there are several Economy seats on the Qantas A380 with unlimited legroom because there is a missing seat in the row in front.

On most large wide-body aircraft, the number of seats by each window reduces from 3 to 2 towards the back of the plane as the fuselage narrows. These seats are great for couples.

Etihad's Boeing 777 has a 3-4-3 layout in most of the Economy cabin, but there are several pairs of two seats at the back of the plane. Photo: Matt Graham.

## How to get the best seats on low-cost carriers

Once you've worked out which is the best seat, the process for claiming it will depend on whether you're flying a low-cost carrier (like Jetstar) or a full-service airline (like Qantas).

Most low-cost carriers will let you choose a seat in advance for a fee. All available seats are usually available for sale on a first-come, first-served basis and the best seats are more expensive. If you're prepared to pay a little more, you can usually choose a good seat.

Seat selection for a Bonza flight.

## How to get the best seats on full-service airlines

Use the "T-80" trick for Qantas seat selection.

The process is a little different on full-service airlines, where you can usually choose a seat in advance for free. But the best seats will be reserved for top-tier frequent flyers.

For example, Qantas blocks the front section of the cabin with these seats unavailable for advance selection (even for a fee) unless you have Platinum One status.

Qantas blocks the seats towards the front of the plane until close to departure, reserving them for frequent flyers. Screenshot from the Qantas website, showing advance seat selection options for a Bronze member.

You can still access those up-front seats! You just need to wait until the airline stops blocking them. With Qantas, this almost always occurs **exactly 80 hours before the flight's scheduled departure time**. That's 3 days and 8 hours in advance.

With Qantas, you'll generally be able to select any remaining seat by logging into "manage booking" exactly 80 hours before the flight. This trick is sometimes known as the "T-80" trick because the seats become available 80 hours prior to take-off.

Other airlines also release blocked seats for selection at a fixed point in time prior to the flight. This won't necessarily be at the 80-hour mark, though. Virgin Australia normally releases all remaining seats for selection when online check-in opens 48 hours before departure. With Singapore Airlines, it's normally around 4 days before the flight.

Often, all remaining seats are released around the time that online check-in opens.

Most full-service airlines now charge a fee for exit row seats. Where this is the case, you'll often be able to select one of these seats from the time of booking by paying the fee.

Some airlines will not allow passengers to select an exit row seat in advance. In this case, these seats will be allocated at airport check-in. If you would like one, get to the airport early to avoid disappointment!

## Beware of aircraft changes

Even the best planning can be undone by last-minute aircraft changes.

For all sorts of operational reasons, airlines might need to "swap out" the aircraft that was planned to operate your flight to a different aircraft with a different seating configuration. When this happens, you may lose your pre-assigned seat/s.

While there's not much you can do about aircraft changes, ExpertFlyer premium subscribers can set an "aircraft change alert" so you'll be notified if this happens - giving you time to choose a new seat before everyone else.

ExpertFlyer lets you set an Aircraft Change Alert.

Using the ExpertFlyer seat alert function mentioned above, you can also set an alert for your own seat. This way, you'll be notified if your allocated seat becomes empty (meaning you've likely been bumped out of it).
