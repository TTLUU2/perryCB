# Introduction to Fare Classes

**Introduction to Fare Classes**

This unit introduces the important concept of fare classes, which will help you immensely with both securing upgrades and finding cheap flights.

## Welcome to our "Everybody Loves an Upgrade" course

This course is dedicated to helping you upgrade to a better experience when you fly. After all, who doesn’t love an upgrade?!

Once upon a time, getting an upgrade to Business or First Class might have involved dressing well or writing a romantic poem for the check-in staff. But despite what you may read online, upgrades are usually awarded based on algorithms rather than a passenger's appearance!

These days, airlines are much less likely to give away upgrades for free. But, the airlines *are* keener than ever to fill their premium cabins in other ways.

All empty seats represent a lost opportunity for the airline. After all, airline seats are highly perishable goods - once the plane has taken off, the airline has no further opportunities to monetise empty seats. This is particularly the case for seats in the pointy end of the plane that could have been sold for a nice premium.

An empty Business Class seat represents a lost revenue opportunity for the airline. Photo: Etihad.

Most airlines would rather earn *something *for their seats than let them fly empty! So, when airlines can’t fill every seat in its premium cabins with customers willing to buy full-priced tickets, this presents some great opportunities to upgrade.

This course is broken down into two modules:

- We'll start by showing you how to secure an upgrade using frequent flyer points on Qantas, Virgin Australia and other airlines.

- In the second module, we'll show you other ways to secure an upgrade. We’ll look at online upgrade auctions, discounted upgrades at check-in, and more.

## Why fare classes matter

To maximise your chances of successfully upgrading flights, and understanding of fare classes is essential. These are technically known in the industry as Reservation Booking Designators (RBDs), and are sometimes also referred to as "fare basis codes", "fare buckets" or "selling classes".

An understanding of fare classes can also help you when looking for frequent flyer reward seats, and more generally when searching for cheap flights.

Understanding fare classes is also important because different fare classes earn frequent flyer points and status credits at different rates, and have different fare conditions attached. For example, tickets booked in some fare classes are ineligible for upgrades.

You can often see the fare class of any itinerary when booking on airline websites. Any good travel agent will also know which fare class they're booking on a given flight. For example, we can see on the Singapore Airlines website that these flights book into "W" class:

## So, what are fare classes?

Fare classes are codes used to identify particular types of airfares available for sale on any given flight. Each is represented by a single letter, such as J, W, Y or X.

On any given flight, there are up to four cabin classes sold - First, Business, Premium Economy and Economy. But there may be over a dozen different *fare *classes available for sale.

Each fare class represents a particular type of fare, for travel in a certain cabin class, at a specific price. Every fare class has a different price point, and the ticket conditions will also vary between classes.

Within the Economy cabin, for example, there are different sub-categories of tickets. Fully flexible Economy fares (generally "Y" class) are the most expensive for that class of travel. But these tickets are generally also the most flexible, with no flight change or cancellation fees. Fully flexible tickets can generally also be upgraded.

There will also be discounted Economy tickets for sale at lower prices and with different fare conditions. For example, these cheaper fares might attract a penalty for changes and could be non-upgradeable.

## What do the letters represent?

Moving from left to right, each letter (fare class) represents the most expensive to the least expensive tickets. Fare class "F", the furthest to the left, is the most expensive First class fare available on the flight. "E", which is furthest to the right, is the cheapest and most restrictive Economy class ticket available.

With Qantas, fare classes "Y" to "E" represent seats in the Economy class cabin. "Y" (sometimes referred to as "yankee" based on the phonetic alphabet), being furthest to the left, represents the most expensive and most flexible Economy fare. A ticket bought in fare class "Y" will also accrue more points and status credits than an "E" or "O" class fare, which would also not be upgradeable (for international flights).

Qantas, like many airlines, uses the "Y" fare class code to denote full-fare Economy. Photo: Qantas.

## What do the numbers represent?

The numbers next to each fare class show the minimum number of seats available for sale right now in that class.

For Qantas flights, 9 is the maximum number of seats that will show as being available in each class. Other airlines may use 4 or 7 as the maximum number. Where 9 is shown, there may be exactly 9 **or more than** 9 seats available for sale in that fare class.

In the example above, there are still at least 9 seats available for sale in every fare class (except "G" class which is reserved for group bookings). This means the flight is probably very lightly booked and there are still many seats available for sale!

There are only a limited number of seats available for sale in each fare class. As the cabin starts to fill up and more cheap seats are sold, the allocations for the cheaper fare classes will become exhausted. As the lower fare classes progressively sell out, fares will increase. This is why flights almost always become more expensive close to the departure date!

If the loadings showed "J3", it would mean there are only 3 Business Class seats left for sale on the flight as "J" is the highest available fare class for Business.

Fare classes allow airlines to control the loads and revenue for each flight. On flights with high demand, airlines will close off the lower fare classes to increase their revenue. Conversely, they may release extra cheap seats to stimulate demand on lightly-loaded flights.

## ExpertFlyer flight availability information is only a guide...

Although the flight availability information shown on Expert Flyer gives a good indication of the number of seats sold on a flight, it doesn't tell us *exactly *how many seats are left. All we know for sure is the minimum number of seats that the airline is currently prepared to sell at each price point. But just because there are 9 "F" and 9 "A" seats available for sale, does not mean there are 18 vacant First Class seats. (This would be impossible as there are only 14 First seats on the Qantas A380.)

Also note that **some airlines practice overselling**. So even if it appears some seats are still available, the airline may be planning to sell more tickets than actual seats available. They can get away with this by upgrading or bumping passengers if needed.
