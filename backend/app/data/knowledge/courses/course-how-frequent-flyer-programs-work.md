## A brief history of frequent flyer programs

Texas International Airlines is credited with launching the world's first frequent flyer program in 1979, although American Airlines AAdvantage is the oldest program that's still around today.

Qantas was the first Australian airline to create a loyalty program in 1987, although it wasn't until the 1990s when frequent flyer programs really took off in Australia.

Launching in 1987, Qantas Frequent Flyer was the first Australian airline loyalty program. Photo: Qantas.

Programs like Qantas Frequent Flyer were originally aimed squarely at high-flying business travellers. They were designed to keep customers loyal to their particular airline, at a time when cheaper airlines were entering the market, by rewarding passengers for their loyalty.

They still do this, but frequent flyer programs today do much more than just rewarding frequent flyers. And the good news is that you no longer have to be a frequent flyer at all to enjoy the rewards!

Most frequent flyer programs have now extended their reach well beyond flying. They have lots of third-party partners, including banks and supermarkets. And in many cases, these programs now make more profit for the airline than the core business of flying passengers!

Today's frequent flyer programs are a "win-win" because they can be extremely valuable for both airlines and members. This may seem like a paradox. But as we'll discover shortly, frequent flyer programs are unique because the rewards on offer can be worth a lot to members - while costing the airline little to provide.

## How frequent flyer programs work

At a basic level, there are 3 aspects to most frequent flyer programs:

**Frequent flyer points are the "currency" of any frequent flyer program.** Points are sometimes called miles, or other program-specific names. For example, British Airways points are known as "Avios".

At a basic level, frequent flyer points can be earned by flying, spending on an affiliated credit card or shopping with program partners. They can then be redeemed for a range of valuable rewards, as offered by the program. These could include things like...

- Reward flights

- Upgrades

- Hotel nights

- Gift cards

- Wine

- And lots more!

Separately, **most airlines also offer multiple elite status tiers** which offer more benefits to frequent flyers at each level. For example, Qantas Frequent Flyer has Silver, Gold, Platinum and Platinum One tiers.

## How frequent flyer programs make money

The above chart shows the basic process of earning and redeeming points in a frequent flyer program from a member's perspective. But how do the loyalty programs make money?

Firstly, airlines sell points to third-party program partners (such as banks and retailers, which offer frequent flyer points to attract and retain their own customers). When a member earns points with a program partner, that partner is paying the airline for those points.

The airline then earns interest on unredeemed points, which are accounted for internally as a liability, until the member either redeems the points for something of value or they expire.

Of course, the airline will always try to ensure the revenue they receive from selling points is higher than the cost to the airline of providing the reward. That's how the frequent flyer program's business model works.

Expired points are known as "breakage" in the industry. Breakage is great for the airline, but not for customers! We'll show you how to ensure your points never expire later in this course.

Earning and redeeming points from a loyalty program's perspective.

As we'll explain in the next unit, the real value is found in redeeming points for things like flights and upgrades. Points can also be redeemed for less exciting rewards like gift vouchers or toasters... but there is much less value in that!

## Why frequent flyer points are valuable

Why collect frequent flyer points? The answer to that is simple.

Think of anywhere in the world that you would love to be right now. Perhaps it's your favourite holiday destination, or a place where you can visit loved ones? Maybe it's a destination you've always dreamed of visiting but never had the opportunity?

Wherever you'd like to go, frequent flyer points won't *just* get you there… they might even be able to get you there in the luxury of Business or First Class! What's more, frequent flyer points often cost nothing or very little to earn. You could be earning points for things you're already doing every day!

With a strategy to earn maximum frequent flyer points and a good understanding of frequent flyer programs, the world is quite literally your oyster.

Dreaming of visiting Paris? You can, thanks to frequent flyer points! Photo: Alexander Kagan on Unsplash.

Loyalty program members don't directly bear the cost of acquiring points. You can easily earn thousands of points for doing things you're already doing anyway, such as shopping or paying for purchases on a credit card or supermarket shopping.

Many Australian Frequent Flyer members have benefited from points enormously over the years. They have allowed people to travel all over the world in comfort, and for very little cost! Over the next 10 training courses, we'll share the tricks of the trade so that you can do the same.

## You can get outsized value from frequent flyer programs

All loyalty programs try to ensure the cost to them of providing a reward is lower than the revenue they earned by issuing the required amount of points. But that doesn't mean you can't get value from frequent flyer programs! Here's why...

The **marginal cost to an airline of providing a reward flight or upgrade is typically very low**, since that seat would have likely otherwise flown empty.

Airline seats are highly perishable goods. Once a flight takes off with an empty seat, there is no possibility for the airline to earn any revenue for it. And the marginal cost of filling another seat on a flight that is already running anyway is very low!

Airlines don't make money by flying empty seats! They would rather fill it and get something for it than nothing. Photo: Matt Graham.

But **the value to a member of a Business or First Class reward seat can be very high**, since the cost of buying an equivalent ticket might be very high.

Airlines control the supply of reward seats and upgrades, and they won't necessarily make a reward available if they could have otherwise sold the seat at full price. This is one of the downsides of frequent flyer programs, but it's also what makes these programs such great value.

This is a key difference between frequent flyer programs and other loyalty programs. With a supermarket loyalty program, for example, it costs the supermarket $10 to provide a gift voucher worth $10. But it might cost an airline $50 to provide a reward that's worth $500 to the member.

The trade-off is that you can very easily redeem your $10 supermarket voucher, and the supermarket can give away an unlimited amount of vouchers. On the other hand, the supply of airline seats is limited, so redeeming points for reward flights can be a bit trickier. A reward seat won't always be available on the flight you want.

That said, airlines of course realise that there needs to be enough reward seats available for their programs to remain attractive. And in Course 5 (Finding & Booking Reward Flights) we'll teach you some helpful strategies to find airline reward seats.
