## How to choose a credit card

Just like when choosing a frequent flyer program, the best way to choose a credit card is to work backwards.

Many people don't put much thought into how they'll spend the points earned on their credit card until they've already got the card and amassed a stash of points. But this *should* be the very *first* step in your decision-making process before choosing a new card!

There is no "one size fits all" credit card. The best one for you will depend on what you want to do with the points, your average spend, any perks you're looking for in a card, and a range of other factors. But to help you decide on a good credit card for you, there are some steps you can follow...

## Determine how you want to spend your points

The first step in choosing a credit card is to determine how you'll eventually want to spend your points. If you want to use them for flights, where do you want to fly to?

## Find the best frequent flyer program for that type of redemption

Once you've worked this out, work out which frequent flyer program is the best value and/or offers the best availability for that type of redemption.

For example, if you mostly travel within Australia, and on Qantas, then Qantas Frequent Flyer is probably your best choice. But if you frequently travel to Asia, then a card that allows you to transfer points to Singapore Airlines KrisFlyer or Cathay may be worth considering.

Cathay Pacific has good award availability from Australia to Asia for Cathay members. Photo: Cathay Pacific.

If you don't yet know how you'll want to spend the points, **the flexibility of a credit card program with many transfer partners - such as American Express Membership Rewards - would be ideal**.

## Find the credit cards that earn points with that program at the highest rate

Once you've worked out which frequent flyer program/s you want to be able to transfer your points to, research which credit cards offer transfers to those programs. Of those cards, which will allow you to earn the most points?

## Consider the extra card benefits and features

Consider the added benefits and perks offered by each of the cards. Perhaps things like lounge access, free flights, travel insurance or hotel status would be valuable to you. If so, then you may want to look at cards that also offer these benefits.

It may be worth paying a higher annual fee for a credit card with benefits such as airport lounge access. Photo: Plaza Premium.

As well as earning points at a higher rate, cards with higher annual fees generally offer better extra benefits. These *alone* can sometimes justify the annual fee! For example, some American Express credit cards come with travel credits worth the same as - or even more than - the card's annual fee.

Some cards do not earn any points at all, but offer fee-free international transactions. This is still a worthwhile benefit if you travel overseas regularly. (We'll cover travel money in lots more detail in Course 10.)

## Look for sign-up bonus points, but beware of high fees

Signing up for a new credit card is a great opportunity to earn bonus points.

If you're just looking for a good sign-up bonus, then the other features of the card may not be so important. But if you're looking for a long-term card to use for your everyday transactions, the sign-up bonus should only be a secondary consideration.

You should also consider the card's annual fee and any other potential downsides.

Finally, check the minimum criteria for applying for any given card. Some cards have minimum income and other requirements that you may need to satisfy.

## Applying for a new card

Once you've worked out *which *credit card/s you want to apply for, the process is fairly simple. You can usually apply online, over the phone, or in person at the bank.

You'll be asked a series of questions about your personal and financial situation, including your income and employment history. While some banks list a minimum income to apply for certain cards, others consider other factors that may impact your ability to pay off the card.

Sometimes your application will be approved instantly. At other times, the bank will ask for supporting documentation such as bank statements or paycheques.

If the bank is satisfied that you meet the minimum criteria and are not a "high risk" customer, you'll be approved and can look forward to your new card arriving in the mail!

As we explained in the previous unit, your credit rating is a key factor in the bank's assessment of your application. Don't get too carried away and apply for too many cards within a short space of time! This may negatively affect your credit rating and jeopardise your access to credit in the future.
