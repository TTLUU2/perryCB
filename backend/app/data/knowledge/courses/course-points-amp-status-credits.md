# Points &amp; Status Credits

## How much are frequent flyer points worth?

As a very general rule, **frequent flyer points are worth around 1 cent each**. But in truth, their value can vary significantly depending on how you use them.

Points in some programs are more valuable than others. But a key factor is how they are spent. For example, Qantas points could be worth as little as 0.5 cents each or as much as 6 cents each.

This means high-value Business & First Class reward flights and upgrades can be worth as much as 12 times more than redemptions at the rewards store.

For example, a Qantas Business Class flight from Sydney to Perth normally costs over $2,500. But you could book a seat for just 43,600 Qantas Frequent Flyer points, plus around $98 in taxes.

Your points will be worth much less if you redeem them for things like gift vouchers or physical products like electrical appliances, which are typically terrible value. Those same 43,600 Qantas points would only be enough to purchase a toaster worth around $218 if redeemed at the Qantas Rewards Store.

Rewards that are not easily converted to cash are the most valuable.

As a general rule, you'll get the best bang for your buck by redeeming your points for rewards that *aren't *easily convertible to cash.

Things like gift cards and appliances can very easily be converted to cash - and they're a very poor value use of points. But airlines can supply an unlimited number of these rewards.

By comparison, upgrading to Business or First class is a great way to spend your points. But airlines can only afford to offer such high-value rewards because they are able to control the supply of them. There are only a certain number of seats on every flight, and airlines are not required to offer them to passengers redeeming points if they can fill them with paying customers. In fact, most airlines will only offer upgrades and award seats if they don't expect to fill the seat with a paying customer.

There are some exceptions, as airlines realise that their frequent flyer program would be worthless if customers were unable to use points on most flights. They don't want to kill their golden goose!

The upshot is that some of those high-value rewards are difficult to obtain. But don't worry - this training program will show you how to get them.

## The problem with "no blackout dates"

If you ever see an airline advertising "no blackout dates on award bookings", this can actually be a bad thing. If they're able to offer you an award seat on any flight, it probably means that they're charging you way too many points!

The cash fare is probably being converted to points at a rate that favours the airline. This is how Qantas Points Plus Pay awards work, and also how the Air New Zealand frequent flyer program works. In our view, Air New Zealand has one of the worst value frequent flyer programs in the world.

## What are status credits?

Frequent flyer points are just one piece of the frequent flyer puzzle.

Most frequent flyer programs use a separate currency to award elite status to loyal flyers. With Qantas Frequent Flyer and Virgin Australia's Velocity Frequent Flyer, "status credits" are used. But other airlines use different terminology, e.g. "tier points", "elite qualifying miles" or some combination of these terms. For simplicity, we'll generally refer to these are "status credits".

**Status credits are used to recognise the airline's most frequent (and high-spending) flyers.** Members of the frequent flyer program can achieve elite status by earning a certain number of status credits each year. The more status credits earned, the higher the member's status level within the program.

Unlike points, status credits can generally only be earned by flying. But there are some exceptions, such as the ability to earn a limited amount of Velocity status credits by shopping at Coles.

Status credits are not a currency and can't be redeemed for anything. They'll also be reset at the end of each membership year. Their sole purpose is to determine a member's status level.

## Airline status

So, why are status credits important? Well, earn enough of them and you'll gain access to some fantastic benefits when you fly!

The idea of elite airline status is to reward the most frequent of flyers with extra benefits, in order to foster ongoing loyalty. Most airlines offer around 3 elite status tiers, with each tier requiring more status credits but coming with progressively greater benefits.

## Glossary of frequent flyer terminology

Throughout the course - and if you participate on forums such as Australian Frequent Flyer - you might come across a range of new terminology.

In case you're unfamiliar with some of the frequent flyer terminology, we've compiled a quick glossary. This list explains the meaning of some of the most common terms and abbreviations.
