# Bonus Sign-Up Points

**Bonus Sign-up Points**

By taking advantage of credit card sign-up bonus offers, you could earn hundreds of thousands of frequent flyer points for minimal outlay!

In this training unit, we will cover…

## About sign-up bonuses

Spending money on your credit card is a good way to accumulate points over a long period of time. But by taking advantage of credit card sign-up bonus points, you can **amass literally tens of thousands of points quickly, and for little or no extra cost**! So if you're serious about earning points, these bonuses will be a massive help to you.

Credit card sign-up bonuses are *the* easiest and cheapest way to earn large quantities of frequent flyer points.

There is a lot of competition in the Australian credit card market. So banks use generous sign-up bonus point offers to attract new customers. Offers are generally between 20,000 and 150,000 points.

100,000 Qantas or Velocity points would be roughly enough for a one-way Business Class flight to Hawaii or Japan, or more than five return Economy flights between Sydney and Melbourne. See our article on how many points you need to book a reward flight for more ideas!

To earn the bonus points on a sign-up offer, you will generally need to pay the card's annual fee. Some banks may also require you to spend a minimum amount of money on the card. For example, you might need to make $3,000 worth of purchases on the card in the first 3 months in order to earn the bonus points. Other offers might simply require you to make *any *purchase within the first 3 months.

## Credit card churning

The credit card churning cycle.

Credit card "churning" involves applying for a new card, receiving the bonus points and then cancelling the card before the next annual fee is due. Serious churners will do this several times per year, continuously earning more and more points through sign-up bonuses.

Credit card churners apply for a new credit card purely for the bonus points - they may keep a small range of cards for everyday use, but most of their cards are cancelled once the bonus points arrive.

There are merits to this strategy, but it is extremely important not to get too carried away. It may be tempting to apply for a dozen credit cards tomorrow, but this could seriously detriment your credit rating.

If you have an excellent credit rating, you may be able to obtain one new credit card every 2-3 months. But if your credit rating is below 700, it would be advisable to leave a longer gap between applications. At the end of the day, **it is not worth damaging your credit rating just for a few free flights**!

## Exclusions for current customers

Most **banks will not award credit card sign-up bonus points to existing cardholders**. If you already have a credit card with a particular bank, you probably won't be eligible for their sign-up offers. You'll also be ineligible for a certain period of time *after* cancelling the card - generally around a year.

Exclusions for existing customers are usually explained in the terms & conditions for each sign-up offer. But here are the standard exclusion periods for the major Australian banks:

## Minimum spend requirements

When applying for a credit card with bonus sign-up points, **you'll generally need to spend a minimum amount within the first 2-3 months to receive the bonus points**. For example, some cards require you to spend $3,000 or $5,000 on purchases eligible to earn points within the first 3 months after approval.

If you have a relatively high monthly credit card spend anyway, you shouldn't have any problems meeting these spend targets. But if you think you might fall short of the spend target, here are some ideas to get over the line without wasting money on unnecessary purchases:

- Pay for as much as you can using your credit card (if you want to maximise your points, you should be paying cash as little as possible anyway!)

- Purchase supermarket gift cards

- Pay bills in advance (you could use a bill payment service such as pay.com.au if you would not ordinarily receive points for your payment, e.g. for ATO payments, or if your biller doesn't normally accept credit card payments)

- Pay rent using a service like Rental Rewards or DEFT (in order to earn points for your payment)

- Use your card to make large purchases on behalf of family or friends (assuming you trust them to pay you back)

- Time a major purchase (e.g. a new computer or car) that you were planning to make anyway so you can count it towards your minimum spend

Before you get started, check the credit card offer terms & conditions carefully. Some cards do not award points for "ineligible" purchases, which could include government payments, gambling transactions and/or prepaid debit cards.

## Some banks drip-feed the bonus points

Although many credit cards still require you to "spend $x within x months" to receive the bonus points, banks are getting more creative.

Some offers now require you to spend a certain amount *each month* for 2, 3 or even 6 months after opening the card. You would receive a portion of the advertised bonus points for each month that you meet the spend target.

Some banks even now require you to keep your card open for at least a second year before awarding the full amount of bonus points. For example, you might get 90,000 bonus points in the first year and an additional 30,000 bonus points if you still have the card in the second year.

When meeting spend targets, make sure that you don't leave your purchases to the last minute. It can take a few days for credit card transactions to process, so purchases made the day before your spend target is due may not count.
